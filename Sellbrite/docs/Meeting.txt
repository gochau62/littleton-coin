Summary
ProfileCoin E-commerce System Challenges
Tyler and Des discussed the current state of ProfileCoin's e-commerce operations and system challenges. Des explained that the company uses Sellbrite as their primary system, with Cellbrite handling inventory management and ShipStation handling shipping, but these systems don't integrate well, requiring extensive manual work and complex spreadsheets to maintain data synchronization. Des described being the primary user of Sellbrite alongside Lee, who is training to take over inventory control functions, while other team members like Hillary, Sue, Dan, and Andrew primarily use the systems for querying rather than day-to-day operations. The discussion revealed that while Sellbrite costs around $7,000 annually, its reporting capabilities are limited, forcing Des to manually export and process raw data to generate accurate sales reports that factor in discounts and taxes.

Cellbrite Product Listing Management
Des explained that while he likes using Cellbrite when it works, he manually removes old product listings from the system every few months to maintain performance, particularly for the "what-you-see-what-you-get" items that build up quickly. He keeps an archive of the purged data in a master VLOOKUP spreadsheet with multiple tabs to enable retrieval of historical information. Des confirmed that Cellbrite handles the current status of products throughout the order process, including payment and shipping status.

Product Upload Platform Process
Tyler and Des discussed the process of uploading products to Cellbrite and various e-commerce platforms. Des explained that after uploading a CSV file to Cellbrite, users must create listings for each product through Cellbrite's internal UI. They noted challenges with listing on Amazon, as it requires creating ASINs manually and cannot be done directly through Cellbrite. Des also mentioned uncertainty about listing on Walmart due to ongoing platform changes. The conversation then shifted to discussing spreadsheets used for managing product data, with Des explaining that the current bulk file format was inspired by eBay and Amazon bulk loaders and aims to consolidate information for all platforms in one place.

Coin Listing Process Workflow
Des explained the process of entering coins into their spreadsheet system, noting that they work with physical products received from Littleton in trays with item numbers and labels. He described how about 80% of listings are common US coins that can be processed efficiently using automated formulas in their spreadsheet, while the remaining 20% consists of more complex items like paper money and foreign coins that require significant research. Des indicated that while they have access to resources like the Redbook and online databases like GraySheet, the current system relies heavily on manual coin knowledge and can become slow when processing non-US items.

Coin Product Input Process
Tyler and Des discussed the process of inputting coin products into their system. Des explained that about a third of their inventory comes certified from major grading companies like NGC and PCGS, while the rest are graded in-house using Littleton specifications. Des estimated that common coin listings take less than a minute per item when using a spreadsheet, but one-off items can take 30 minutes to an hour due to research requirements. The team processes multiple batches of 30-50 coins daily, with Des updating dropdown options almost daily and making major product additions a few times per month.

Backend System Updates and Management
Des explained that he regularly makes minor tweaks to the backend system, including adding new coin denominations, varieties, and handling formatting changes based on platform updates like Amazon's character limit reduction. He estimated that about 80% of the time he relies on formulas for common US coins, while manually customizing descriptions for foreign, ancient, or unique items. Des clarified that product distribution channels are determined by Hillary, Sue, and Andrew, with most products going to all stores unless specifically marked as website exclusive, though communication about exclusivity could be improved.

Amazon Product Listing Management Process
Des explained the process of managing product listings for Amazon, noting that uploads happen twice a month and involve syncing around 300-500 SKUs. He described how Cellebrite handles inventory and price syncing but lags behind other platforms like eBay in updating changes. Des also detailed the use of a "don't list on Amazon" filter and various flags in a spreadsheet to manage product categories and ensure compliance with Amazon's requirements, including handling specific fields like dates and mint marks.

Amazon and eBay Upload Systems
Des explained that while they would prefer a "set and forget" system for Amazon uploads, the current process requires manual control due to the need to account for various data anomalies and formatting requirements. Des noted that Amazon's required values have never changed in the 7 years they've been using the system, though they remain very specific about formatting. For eBay Live listings, Des described how a simplified spreadsheet was created to handle the unique requirements of live streaming sales, which requires minimal product details but has become increasingly complex over time to address tax compliance issues.

eBay Live Show Process Discussion
Tyler and Des discussed the process of live shows, where Des confirmed they conduct one show per day with approximately 150 items per show, typically selected by the management team and host with flexibility for adjustments. Des explained that products uploaded for eBay Live are exclusive to that platform and don't transfer to other sales channels like the website or Amazon, though unsold items may be reassigned new SKUs if they're moved to different sales channels. Des clarified that while he manages the Cellbrite system for eBay Live, he doesn't have detailed knowledge of what happens to unsold products after they're removed from eBay Live.

Image Processing and Pricing Workflow
Tyler and Des discussed the image processing workflow, where photos of products are taken and edited using GIMP, with file naming following specific SKU patterns to ensure proper image-to-product linking. Des explained the pricing process, noting that while buyers previously set retail prices, the company now handles pricing independently using various data points including cost averages from the AS400 system and market values from resources like the gray sheet. The team uses a complex spreadsheet to calculate retail prices, with Des expressing a desire for automation of this process in the future.
